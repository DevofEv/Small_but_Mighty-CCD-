The Design Challenge: Making Democracy Work for Everyone

This "Small but Mighty" exploration, a project by the Center for Civic Design, delves into how even local, small-scale initiatives can have an outsized impact on democratic participation. We examine real-world case studies to uncover what works, what challenges arise, and how these innovative forms of engagement contribute to more responsive and equitable policymaking.

Our goal is to connect research findings with practical lessons, showing policymakers and civic professionals how thoughtfully designed public participation can strengthen democracy from the ground up.

Participatory Budgeting (PB)

Design Problem: Public budgets don't always reflect community needs.

Design Solution: Participatory Budgeting (PB) is designed to give residents direct power in deciding how public money is spent. It's a community-led process from idea generation, through volunteer-led proposal development, to a community-wide vote.

Evanston, IL: Designing for Equitable Recovery

Evanston used $3 million in ARPA funds, with expertise from Northwestern University aiding process design. Design challenge: ensure COVID-19 recovery funds reached underserved communities.

"The historic level of turnout... is another demonstration of Evanston's passionate and engaged citizenry... Our residents' ideas and involvement are truly our greatest asset." - Mayor Daniel Biss

Design Choice: Inclusive voting (age 14+, no citizenship needed) and widespread outreach tapped into a "pent-up desire to shape the city's recovery," leading to 8.5% voter turnout (6,565 residents).

Gibsons, BC: Designing for Consensus

This small town (pop. ~5,000) designed a CA with SFU's RPHI for its OCP (growth & housing). One councillor noted the CA provided crucial confirmation of public support for ideas, lending them greater legitimacy than traditional hearings.

  • Design Choice: 25 members by civic lottery.
  • Met 30-40 hours; stipends ensured participation.
  • Produced 6 key recommendations (75% agreement), including a Community Values Framework.
  • Council prioritized all recommendations.

Design transformed divisive planning into constructive solutions, with positive local media like "Quite proud of this little town."

Eugene, OR: Navigating Housing Laws

Eugene (pop. ~177,000) designed a 29-member Panel with Healthy Democracy for "Middle Housing" law. The panel helped proactively address community concerns, contributing to less acrimonious policy adoption.

  • Design Choice: Civic lottery, ensuring diversity (age, race, renter/owner).
  • Met online 35 hours; stipends provided.
  • Produced 4 reports with recommendations.
  • Panel's input influenced final city policies, balancing diverse views.

CA design legitimized tough choices by including diverse voices. The city reported it "engaged a wider range of community members than any previous planning effort."

Civic Technology

Design Problem: How can technology make participation more accessible and effective?

Design Solution: Thoughtfully designed digital tools can inform citizens and lower barriers. Good design must prioritize equity, accessibility (e.g., WCAG compliance), and data privacy.

Motivote: Designing for Youth Voter Engagement

Web app for college students (18-29) using gamification to simplify voter prep, reducing "friction" and lowering the "activation energy" for participation.

A Minnesota college official noted Motivote led to their "best semester for voter engagement."

A Call to Action: Designing a More Participatory Future

The "Small but Mighty" initiatives show that practical, well-designed tools can revitalize civic life and build stronger, fairer democracies. These are not just idealistic experiments; they are practical tools that can be adapted and scaled. The power to design a better future is in our communities.

Let's invest in designing participatory systems, embrace thoughtful discussion, prioritize inclusion in our designs, and create a culture of learning to unlock this potential. When institutions hand over a bit of power, people prove themselves capable and eager to use it responsibly.
