Participatory Budgeting (PB)

Design Problem: Public budgets don't always reflect community needs.

Design Solution: Participatory Budgeting (PB) is designed to give residents direct power in deciding how public money is spent. It's a process involving idea generation, community-led proposal development, and voting, leading to real local improvements.

Evanston, IL: Designing for Equitable Recovery

Evanston used $3 million in federal ARPA funds for its first PB cycle. The design challenge: ensure COVID-19 recovery funds reached underserved communities.

Design Choice: Inclusive voting rules (age 14+, no citizenship needed) led to 8.5% voter turnout (6,565 residents), far above the typical 1% for PB in the U.S.

Gibsons, BC: Designing for Consensus in Planning

This small town (pop. ~5,000) designed a CA to inform its Official Community Plan, focusing on growth and housing.

  • Design Choice: 25 members selected by civic lottery to mirror the community.
  • Met for 30-40 hours; received stipends to ensure participation.
  • Produced 6 key recommendations with 75% agreement.
  • Town Council prioritized all recommendations for the plan.

This design transformed potentially divisive planning into constructive, community-backed solutions.

Eugene, OR: Designing to Navigate Housing Laws

Eugene (pop. ~177,000) designed a 29-member Citizen Review Panel to advise on Oregon's "Middle Housing" law.

  • Design Choice: Selected by civic lottery, ensuring diversity (age, race, renter/owner).
  • Met online for 35 hours; received stipends.
  • Produced 4 reports with recommendations.
  • Panel's input influenced final city policies by balancing diverse views.

The CA design helped legitimize tough policy choices by ensuring diverse voices, like renters, were part of the solution-building process.

Civic Technology

Design Problem: How can technology make participation more accessible and effective?

Design Solution: Thoughtfully designed digital tools can inform citizens and lower barriers to participation. However, good design must prioritize equity and accessibility.

Motivote: Designing for Youth Voter Engagement

This web app is designed for college students (ages 18-29). It uses game-like features (points, badges, team competitions) to simplify voter prep and build positive voting habits.

The pie chart conceptually shows Motivote's focus on young voters, a group that often has lower turnout. The design aims to make voting prep engaging for them.

üéØ The platform's design, using behavioral nudges, aims to increase turnout up to 10%.

Deployed at universities like U. of Houston, it's a cost-effective design for outreach.

Design Principles for Better Civic Outcomes

Effective civic engagement isn't an accident; it's the result of careful design. These principles guide initiatives that truly make an impact.

üåç

Design for Inclusion

Actively design to include and empower diverse voices, especially from marginalized groups (e.g., easy voting, stipends, language support).

üîç

Design for Transparency

Ensure all parts of the process are open, understandable, and accessible (e.g., clear rules, public information).

üéØ

Design with Clear Purpose

Clearly define the goals and how citizen input will influence decisions to set realistic expectations.

üí∞

Design with Resources

Commit enough funding, staff, and tech support for a high-quality process.

Design for Fair Discussion

Use skilled, neutral facilitators to guide discussions and ensure respectful dialogue.

ü§ù

Design for Trust

Create processes that are fair, transparent, responsive, and respect citizen contributions.

üîÑ

Design for Impact

Clearly show how input will be used by decision-makers and report back on results.

üß†

Design for Learning

Provide time, balanced information, and structured ways for people to learn and discuss options.

üìà

Design for Improvement

Regularly check how well processes work and use feedback to make them better.
